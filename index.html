"Couldn't a good team just move faster on the traditional path?"
A fair challenge — here's why the delays are structural, not a discipline problem
01
Language is lossy
AHA or ADO tickets mean different things to different readers. The first design review reveals those gaps — but by then you've spent weeks writing and scheduling. A prototype is unambiguous from day one.
02
Dependencies are sequential and load-bearing
Engineering waits on design, which waits on tickets, which waits on stakeholder alignment. Each handoff adds weeks. The Fast Track collapses those gates — misalignment surfaces when only hours are at stake, not after weeks of build.
🎨
"How UX and Design's role changes in this model"
Here's how the Fast Track complements rather than replaces design thinking
01
The prototype is a question, not an answer
A Figma Make prototype isn't a final design decision — it's a tangible hypothesis. It makes the problem visible rather than abstract. Design's role shifts from translating a written brief into pixels, to interrogating a working prototype and identifying what it gets wrong. That's still design thinking — applied to a richer starting point.
02
Design depth happens in Phase 2 and beyond
The Fast Track doesn't skip UX rigour — it front-loads alignment so designers spend their expertise on validated problems. Rather than spending weeks crafting a solution to a brief that turns out to be the wrong problem, designers enter at Phase 2 with real user reactions in hand. Their craft goes further because it's applied to signal, not assumption.
Initiate
01
Parallel Prototyping in Figma Make
Figma Make
Non-technical builders (PMs, designers, founders) kick off 2–3 parallel prototype variants in Figma Make simultaneously. The goal isn't a finished product — it's to externalize thinking into working interactive prototypes rather than documents. Each variant tests a different architectural or UX assumption. Time: 2–4 hours.
⏱ Hours, not weeks
👤 Non-technical builder
📦 Output: working app variants
Validate
02
Validate with Design and Engineering
Demo, Don't Memo
Show a working prototype, not a long written ticket or a slide deck for internal team meetings and stakeholder presentations, like SteerCo or Sprint demos. Stakeholders react to real interactions, not imagined ones. This collapses interpretation gaps — everyone aligns on the same artifact. Feedback is concrete and actionable. One winning prototype is selected.
🎯 Select 1 winner
🗣 Stakeholders react to reality

Handoff
03
Code Export & Engineering Handoff
Version Control
The validated Figma Make prototype is exported as a working codebase and handed to engineering. Engineers receive real UI, real routes, and real logic — however rough. The artifact replaces the document. Guesswork is eliminated before engineering begins.
📁 Real code, not a mockup

Engineers open the codebase in their preferred AI-assisted coding environment. Their job shifts from building what was described to connecting the pipes — adding auth, APIs, error handling, performance, security, and scale on top of a proven UX skeleton. The prototype is the spec. Engineering time is dramatically compressed.

Production Deploy
Production
The product ships faster and with less rework risk. Stakeholders already reacted to the real experience — alignment happened at the prototype stage, not during QA or UAT. Because misalignment was caught when only hours were at stake, late-stage surprises are rare. The team ships what was actually validated, not what was imagined in a document.
🚀 Ship with confidence
♻️ Loop back cheaply
⚡
The Core Inversion
Traditional development treats the prototype as a luxury you do after alignment. The Fast Track treats the prototype as the instrument of alignment. The delays in the traditional path aren't a people problem — they're structural. Language is lossy, calendars create friction, and engineering can't start until every upstream dependency is resolved. The Fast Track eliminates those dependencies by making the vision tangible before any process begins. The artifact replaces the document. Non-technical people building the skeleton isn't a workaround — it's the feature. Engineers stop translating vision and start engineering execution. That's where the time savings compound.
