The Core Shift
Expectations are rising. Timelines are compressing. The ask — more output, faster delivery, leaner execution — isn't going away. What has changed is what's now possible: AI has made it viable to build and validate a working product idea in days, not weeks. The question is whether the process around it is built to use that advantage.

The Fast Track is built for exactly that. Rather than treating the prototype as something you create after alignment, it makes the prototype the instrument of alignment. The vision is tangible before any downstream work begins. Engineers start from a proven skeleton, not a written brief. The artifact replaces the document. Speed compounds — and craft, collaboration, and quality are fully preserved.

Initiate
01
Parallel Prototyping in Figma Make
Figma Make
Non-technical builders — PMs, product owners, and business stakeholders — kick off 2–3 parallel prototype variants in Figma Make simultaneously. The goal isn't a finished product — it's to externalize thinking into working interactive prototypes rather than documents. Each variant tests a different architectural or UX assumption. Time: 2–5 days.
⏱ Days, not weeks

Validate with Design, Engineering & Stakeholders
Demo, Don't Memo
Show a working prototype, not a long written ticket or a slide deck for internal team meetings and stakeholder presentations, like SteerCo or Sprint demos. Stakeholders react to real interactions, not imagined ones. This collapses interpretation gaps — everyone aligns on the same artifact. Feedback is concrete and actionable. One winning prototype is selected.

03
Design Refinement & System Application
Figma · Design System
With a validated prototype and real stakeholder reactions in hand, this is where UX and design craft is applied: polishing interaction patterns, applying the design system, ensuring accessibility standards, and producing the final visual specification for engineering. Design is working from a position of validated insight rather than assumption.
🎨 Apply design system

Engineer
04
Engineering & Production Build
AI-Assisted Coding
With the final design spec in hand and a working prototype already providing reusable code, engineering can move significantly faster. Rather than building from a blank canvas, engineers reference and adapt the prototype's existing UI, routes, and logic — then add what makes it production-ready: authentication, APIs, error handling, performance, accessibility compliance, analytics, and scale. The guesswork is gone. The vision was validated before a single line of production code was written.
♻️ Reusable prototype code

Production Deploy
Production
The product ships faster and with less rework risk. Stakeholders already reacted to the real experience — alignment happened at the prototype stage, not during QA or UAT. Because misalignment was caught when only hours were at stake, late-stage surprises are rare. The team ships what was actually validated, not what was imagined in a document.

"Couldn't a good team just move faster on the traditional path?"
A fair challenge — here's why the delays are structural, not a discipline problem
01
Language is lossy
AHA or ADO tickets mean different things to different readers. The first design review reveals those gaps — but by then you've spent weeks writing and scheduling. A prototype is unambiguous from day one.
02
Dependencies are sequential and load-bearing
Engineering waits on design, which waits on tickets, which waits on stakeholder alignment. Each handoff adds weeks. The Fast Track collapses those gates — misalignment surfaces when only hours are at stake, not after weeks of build.
🎨
"How UX and Design's role changes in this model"
Here's how the Fast Track complements rather than replaces design thinking
01
The prototype is a question, not an answer
A Figma Make prototype isn't a final design decision — it's a tangible hypothesis. It makes the problem visible rather than abstract. Design's role shifts from translating a written brief into pixels, to interrogating a working prototype and identifying what it gets wrong. That's still design thinking — applied to a richer starting point.
02
Design depth happens in Phase 2 and beyond
The Fast Track doesn't skip UX rigour — it front-loads alignment so designers spend their expertise on validated problems. Rather than spending weeks crafting a solution to a brief that turns out to be the wrong problem, designers engage at Phase 3 with real stakeholder reactions in hand. Their craft goes further because it's applied to signal, not assumption.
